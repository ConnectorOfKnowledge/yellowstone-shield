You're going to Yellowstone. Know this.

  1. Bears: Make noise on trails. Never run. Back away slowly. If a grizzly charges, play dead face-down. If a black bear attacks, fight back.
  2. Bison: Stay 25+ yards away. They're faster than you. If one charges, get behind a large object (tree, car). Never approach for a selfie.
  3. Moose: Give them space. If a moose lowers its head and pins its ears back, it's about to charge. Get behind a tree or large object.
  4. Carry bear spray ($40-50 at any outdoor store in Montana). Keep it accessible, not buried in your pack.
More people are injured by bison in Yellowstone than by any other animal. They look calm. They are not. 25 yards minimum.
  1. Pull over immediately. Turn off the engine.
  2. Get everyone out and at least 100 feet away.
  3. Do NOT open the hood -- oxygen feeds the fire.
  4. Call 911. Do not attempt to fight the fire unless it's tiny and you have an extinguisher.
  5. Warn oncoming traffic if safe to do so.
Cars can explode. Distance is safety. Do not go back for belongings.
  1. Get to a sturdy building immediately. Lowest floor, interior room, away from windows.
  2. If caught in your car: Do NOT try to outrun it. Park, keep seatbelt on, duck below the windows, cover your head.
  3. If in open country: find a ditch or low area, lie flat face-down, cover your head.
  4. Do NOT shelter under an overpass. Wind accelerates through them.
You'll cross through Nebraska, South Dakota, and Montana -- tornado country. Keep weather alerts on your phone.
  1. Pull over if visibility drops or hail starts. Get under a gas station canopy or overpass if possible.
  2. Turn on hazards. Stay in your car with seatbelt on.
  3. Stay away from flooded roads. "Turn around, don't drown" -- 6 inches of water can sweep a car.
  4. If lightning is close: stay in your car (the metal frame protects you, not the tires).
  1. Reduce speed significantly. Leave 3x normal following distance.
  2. If you can't see: pull off the road completely. Turn on hazards. Wait it out.
  3. If you slide: take your foot off the gas. Steer in the direction you want to go. Do NOT slam the brakes.
  4. If stranded: stay in the car. Run the engine 10 minutes per hour for heat (crack a window). Tie something bright to the antenna.
  5. Keep the exhaust pipe clear of snow to prevent carbon monoxide buildup inside the car.
Montana mountain passes can get snow even in late spring. Check road conditions at 511mt.net before heading through passes.
  1. Grip the wheel firmly with both hands. Expect gusts when passing trucks or exiting tunnels.
  2. Reduce speed. High-profile vehicles (trucks, RVs) can tip in 60+ mph winds.
  3. Watch for blowing debris on the road -- tumbleweeds, branches, sand.
  4. If winds are extreme (60+ mph): pull over in a sheltered spot and wait.
Wyoming and Montana are notoriously windy, especially I-90. This app's weather bar warns you about high winds before you leave.
